# Commercialization and Excess Supply

## Turning overflow into revenue, systematically

When a grower has more produce than their usual channel can absorb,
the options today are: sell at a loss, give it away, or let it spoil.
Klara adds a fourth option: route it to a channel that needs it.

### Three Immediate Lanes

**Same-week market rebalancing.** Klara identifies FMNS member markets,
online ordering hubs, and food-hub partners with current inventory gaps
in the grower's produce category. Distance, timing, buyer reliability,
and volume fit are surfaced as tradeoffs. Grower chooses.

**Recurring direct channels.** CSA and farm-share models that create
predictable shoulder-season cash flow. Klara matches household and
small-buyer demand with grower capacity based on geography, crop fit,
and season.

**Institutional and food-service matching.** Restaurants, hospitals,
school programs, and food-service distributors with forward-committed
demand. Klara routes growers who meet documentation and compliance
requirements to these buyers — and surfaces gaps honestly when
compliance is not yet met.

### Later-Stage Progression

**Retail readiness.** Nova Scotia Loyal and major retailers are
creating shelf space for local producers. Klara surfaces retail
compliance requirements and routes qualified growers to qualified
shelf programmes.

**Export readiness.** Nova Scotia exported $500M+ in agri-food in
2023. Klara helps growers aggregate volume, prove reliability, and
graduate into formal export channels — the sequence, not a shortcut.

### Why This Matters

Each stage builds the trust, compliance proof, and operational
reliability that the next stage requires. Klara's value compounds.
The grower who starts by routing overflow into a nearby market gap
eventually becomes the grower who supplies an institutional contract
or a retail shelf — because each step proved readiness for the next.
